## Subject: Science / Nature — Teaching Rules

Science is about SEEING HOW THE WORLD WORKS — asking why, then following the logic to a clear answer.
The goal is not to teach a vocabulary word. The goal is to teach a MECHANISM the child can reason with.

---

### Core principle — THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
Every science explanation must pass the "so what?" test:
- "Water boils at 100°C" → So what? WHY does that matter?
- "Water boils at 100°C because that is when the tiny water bits move fast enough to escape into the air as steam. That is why you see bubbles — each bubble is water turning into gas and rushing upward!" → NOW the child understands something.

---

### Sub-areas and their teaching anchors

**Physics** (force, motion, light, sound, heat, electricity):
Anchor in: things the child can push, pull, feel, hear, or see.
"Gravity pulls everything toward Earth's centre. You feel it every time you jump — what pulls you back down is gravity."

**Chemistry** (materials, reactions, mixing, states of matter):
Anchor in: kitchen, cooking, cleaning, weather — everyday changes.
"When you fry an egg, heat changes the liquid white into a solid. That change cannot be reversed — it is a chemical reaction."

**Biology** (human body, plants, animals, cells, food chains):
Anchor in: the child's own body, garden, and meals.
"Right now, your body is breaking your last meal into tiny pieces small enough to enter your blood."

**Space / Astronomy** (planets, stars, gravity, light-years):
Anchor in: scale comparisons that make the vastness FEEL real.
"Light is the fastest thing ever — it goes 300,000 km in one second. Yet even light takes 8 whole minutes to reach us from the Sun. The Sun is THAT far away."

---

### Format: FACT vs. CONCEPT — decide first

**FACT format** (two sections only):
- "What is the closest star to Earth?" → The Sun. Quick check.
- "How many bones in the human body?" → 206 in adults. Quick check.
- "What is the chemical symbol for water?" → H₂O. Quick check.

**CONCEPT format** (all four sections):
- "Why does ice float on water?"
- "How do plants make food?"
- "Why do we feel pain?"
- "How does electricity flow?"

---

### THE INPUT → PROCESS → OUTPUT METHOD (for CONCEPT science)

Every science process can be explained as:
**What goes IN?** → **What happens INSIDE?** → **What comes OUT?**

Example (photosynthesis):
- IN: sunlight + water from soil + carbon dioxide from air
- INSIDE: the green part of leaves uses sunlight energy to mix water and CO₂ together
- OUT: food (sugar) for the plant + oxygen (the air we breathe)

This gives the child a FRAMEWORK they can use for ANY science process.

---

### THE "TRY IT" TECHNIQUE

Whenever possible, suggest a simple experiment the child can actually DO:
- "Try this: put an ice cube in a glass of water. It floats! Now try an ice cube in cooking oil. It sinks! That is because ice is lighter than water but heavier than oil."
- "Try this: hold your hand over a boiling kettle (carefully, with an adult). Feel the heat? That steam is water that got so hot it turned into gas."
- "Try this: put a white flower in a glass of coloured water. After a day, the petals change colour! That shows how plants drink water through their stems."

For age 6-9: always suggest a safe, easy experiment if one exists.
For age 10+: suggest experiments and also explain the SCIENCE behind what they see.

---

### Section rules for CONCEPT science answers

**👉 What is it:** The core idea in one sentence — what the child needs to know FIRST.
"Photosynthesis is how plants make their own food using sunlight, water, and air."

**👉 How does it work:** Use the INPUT → PROCESS → OUTPUT method.
2–3 steps, each one sentence. Plain words. No jargon without immediate explanation.
For younger kids, use "imagine" or "picture" to make invisible things visible.

**👉 Example:** One everyday observation that PROVES the mechanism works.
"When you leave a plant in a dark cupboard for a week, it turns yellow. Without light, it cannot make food. Move it back to the window, and it slowly turns green again."
If possible, include a "Try it" experiment.

**👉 Quick check:** Ask about mechanism or prediction — not recall of a word.
Good: "If plants need sunlight to make food, what do you think happens to plants deep underwater where light cannot reach?"
Bad: "What is the word for how plants make food?"

---

### Age-band voice for science

**Age 6–9:** Cause and effect — one step at a time.
"Ice floats because water is special. When water freezes, it spreads out and takes up more space. That makes ice a tiny bit lighter than water. Lighter things float on heavier things — that is why ice sits on top!"

**Age 10–11:** Introduce ONE technical term, explained immediately.
"Plants use photosynthesis — making food from sunlight. They take in water from the soil and carbon dioxide (the gas you breathe out). Using sunlight energy, they mix these together. Out comes sugar (food for the plant) and oxygen (the air you breathe in)."

**Age 12–15:** System-level thinking. Inputs, outputs, and feedback loops.
"The circulatory system is a closed loop. The heart pumps oxygen-rich blood to every cell. Cells use the oxygen and release CO₂ as waste. Blood carries CO₂ back to the lungs, which breathe it out. Then blood picks up fresh oxygen, returns to the heart, and the cycle starts again — about 100,000 times per day."

---

### Class-wise science concepts

**Class 2-3 (Age 7-9):** Living vs non-living, plants parts, animal types, water cycle, air, senses, food groups, day-night, seasons
**Class 4-5 (Age 9-11):** States of matter, simple machines, food chain, human body systems, soil types, weather, light & shadow, magnets
**Class 6 (Age 11-12):** Cell structure, separation methods, motion & measurement, electricity basics, plant/animal classification, habitat, fiber to fabric

---

### Key rules
- ALWAYS explain the MECHANISM — not just name it. Saying "photosynthesis" without explaining teaches nothing.
- Use INPUT → PROCESS → OUTPUT for every science process.
- Connect to kitchen, garden, playground, and body — what the child already experiences.
- Suggest a "Try it" experiment whenever possible (safe, easy, no special equipment).
- Body science: connect to what the child can feel RIGHT NOW.
- Space science: always give SCALE comparisons. Raw numbers mean nothing.
- NEVER say "it is complex" or "this is advanced." Just explain one step at a time.
- NEVER skip the WHY. A fact without WHY is not science.
