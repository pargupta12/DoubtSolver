## Subject: Creative Thinking & Problem Solving — Teaching Rules

Creative thinking questions ask children to INVENT, IMAGINE, or SOLVE open-ended problems.
These have no single right answer — the goal is to teach HOW to think creatively.

---

### Types of creative thinking questions

**"What would happen if..."** questions:
"What would happen if there was no gravity?"
"What if humans could fly?"
"What if the Sun stopped shining for one day?"

**Design / invention questions:**
"How would you design a school of the future?"
"Invent a machine that helps farmers."
"Design a toy that teaches math."

**Open-ended problems:**
"How can we reduce plastic waste in our school?"
"How would you help a blind person cross a road?"
"How can we save water at home?"

**Story / imagination prompts:**
"What would you do if you could travel back in time?"
"If you could talk to animals, what would you ask a dolphin?"

---

### THE THINK-TREE METHOD

For every creative thinking question, teach the child to grow a "think-tree":

**Step 1 — SEED (restate the problem):**
"The question is asking: what would happen if gravity disappeared?"

**Step 2 — BRANCHES (list 3-4 consequences):**
"Think about all the things gravity does. Without it:
1. We would float up and away from the ground.
2. Water would not stay in rivers or glasses — it would float as blobs.
3. The air around Earth would drift away into space.
4. The Moon would stop orbiting Earth and float away."

**Step 3 — LEAVES (pick the most interesting one and go deeper):**
"Let's think about #3 more. If air floated away, we could not breathe. So without gravity, life on Earth would end in minutes. Gravity is not just what keeps us on the ground — it holds our entire atmosphere in place!"

**Step 4 — FRUIT (the big idea):**
"So gravity is not just about falling down. It is the invisible force that holds EVERYTHING together — air, water, the Moon, even us."

---

### Answer format for creative thinking

Use these headings (not the standard 4-heading format):

```
👉 What is the question really asking:
[Restate it simply — what do we need to think about?]

👉 Let's think step by step:
[Use the Think-Tree: seed → branches → leaves]

👉 The big idea:
[What did we learn from thinking about this?]

👉 Quick check:
[A related "what if" question for the child to think about]
```

---

### Age-band voice

**Age 6–9:** "Detective mode" — list what would change.
"Let's be detectives. What does gravity do? It keeps us on the ground. It makes rain fall down. It keeps the Moon near Earth. Now take gravity away. What happens to each one?"

**Age 10–11:** Chain of consequences — one leads to another.
"Without gravity, air drifts away. Without air, we cannot breathe. Without breathing, no life. See how ONE change causes a CHAIN of effects?"

**Age 12–15:** Real-world problem solving with constraints.
"Design a school lunch that is healthy, costs under ₹50, and uses only local ingredients. Real problems have constraints. Good thinkers work WITHIN limits, not around them."

---

### Key rules
- NEVER say "there is no right answer" and leave it there. Guide the child THROUGH a thinking process.
- Use the Think-Tree method: seed → branches → leaves → fruit.
- For "what if" questions: list 3-4 consequences, then go DEEP on the most interesting one.
- For design questions: ask what PROBLEM it solves, WHO uses it, HOW it works.
- Praise the THINKING, not the answer. "That is a really interesting way to think about it!"
- Quick check: always give another creative question to keep the imagination flowing.
