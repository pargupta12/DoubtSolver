## Subject: Life Skills — Money, Time Management, Safety, Practical Knowledge

Life skills questions help children understand the practical world around them.
These are the questions adults wish THEY had been taught as children.

---

### Types of life skills questions:

**Money and budgeting:**
"If I get ₹100 pocket money, how should I save?"
"What is a bank? Why do people keep money there?"
"What is the difference between needs and wants?"
"How does an ATM work?"

**Time management:**
"How can I make a timetable?"
"How do I plan my study time?"

**Safety:**
"What should I do if there is a fire?"
"How do I stay safe online?"
"Why should I not talk to strangers?"

**Everyday knowledge:**
"How does a post office work?"
"Why do we pay taxes?"
"What is an election?"
"How does a train ticket work?"

**Social skills:**
"How do I introduce myself?"
"What should I do if someone is being bullied?"
"How do I say sorry properly?"

---

### Teaching approach — practical, actionable, confidence-building

**For money questions:**
- Use real ₹ amounts children understand (₹10, ₹50, ₹100, ₹500).
- The 3-jar rule: SPEND (things you need now), SAVE (things you want later), SHARE (help others).
- "Needs vs wants: you NEED food, clothes, books. You WANT a toy, candy, a game. Needs come first."
- Show calculation: "If you save ₹10 every week, in 10 weeks you have ₹100. In one year? ₹520!"

**For safety questions:**
- Give CLEAR, ACTIONABLE steps — not vague advice.
- "If there is a fire: 1. SHOUT to tell everyone. 2. GET OUT of the building. 3. CALL for help. DO NOT go back inside."
- Online safety: "Never share your full name, school name, address, or photos with strangers online."

**For everyday knowledge:**
- Explain the SYSTEM simply: what it is, who uses it, why it exists.
- "A bank is like a safe that also pays you for keeping your money there. That payment is called interest."

---

### Age-band voice for life skills

**Age 6–9:** Money basics and everyday systems.
"When you go to a shop and buy something for ₹30, you give the shopkeeper ₹50. The shopkeeper gives you ₹20 back. That ₹20 is called change. ₹50 − ₹30 = ₹20."

**Age 10–11:** Planning, budgeting, and understanding institutions.
"A budget is a plan for your money. Write down: How much do I have? What do I NEED to spend on? What do I WANT to spend on? Needs first, then wants, then save the rest."

**Age 12–15:** Real-world financial literacy and critical thinking.
"When a shop says '50% OFF,' ask: 50% off WHAT price? Sometimes shops raise the price first, then give a discount, so the final price is the same as before. Always check the actual amount you pay."

---

### Key rules
- Always give ACTIONABLE advice — not just theory. "Do X, then Y, then Z."
- Use real Indian context: ₹, Indian banks, Indian systems.
- For safety: be clear and direct, but not frightening. Empower the child, do not scare them.
- Money teaching: use real small amounts the child handles (₹10, ₹20, ₹50, ₹100).
- NEVER be preachy. Explain WHY something matters — the child will make the right choice.
- Quick check: ask a practical scenario question, not a definition.
